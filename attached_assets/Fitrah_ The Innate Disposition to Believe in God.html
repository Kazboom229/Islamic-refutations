What is Fitrah?

Fitrah (فطرة) is a fundamental Islamic concept referring to the pure, natural state in which Allah creates every human being with an innate disposition to believe in and worship Him.

In Islamic theology, fitrah signifies:

  • The primordial covenant made between Allah and all human souls
  • The natural inclination toward tawhid (monotheism) present in every person
  • A universal truth that transcends cultural, geographical, and historical differences
  • The pure state that can be obscured by negative environmental influences

"So direct your face toward the religion, inclining to truth. [Adhere to] the fitrah of Allah upon which He has created [all] people." (Quran 30:30)

Fitrah (الفطرة) encompasses multiple dimensions radiating from the core of human nature

The Petrovich Experiments

Dr. Olivera Petrovich, developmental psychologist at Oxford University, conducted groundbreaking research on children's concepts of natural and supernatural causality.

Key findings:

  • Children from non-religious backgrounds still attribute creation to God
  • Young children appear to have a "natural theism"
  • These tendencies appear across diverse cultural contexts

Children naturally perceive design and purpose in the world

Petrovich's Experimental Design

1. Participants

- British group: 38 children (ages 4-7) from varied religious backgrounds

- Japanese group: 42 children (ages 4-7) with minimal exposure to monotheistic concepts

2. Control Variables

- Matching for age, socioeconomic status, and cognitive development

- Careful translation of questions to preserve conceptual equivalence

3. Materials

- Set of 14 high-quality photographs of natural objects (mountains, lakes, stars)

- Set of 8 photographs of human-made objects (buildings, toys, clothing)

4. Procedure

- Individual interviews in familiar settings (schools)

- Questions asked: "How did this object get here?" and "How did it get made?"

Petrovich's Experimental Process

Example Interview Protocol:

Interviewer: "Look at this picture of mountains. How do you think the mountains got here?"

Child (Japanese, age 5): "God made them."

Interviewer: "How did God make them?"

Child: "God has big power. God can make everything."

Interviewer: "Now look at this bicycle. How did the bicycle get here?"

Child: "People made it in a factory with tools and machines."

Key Results:

  • 91% of Japanese children attributed the origin of natural objects to God/divine action
  • Nearly all children correctly attributed human-made objects to human designers
  • Children consistently distinguished between human and divine creativity
  • No significant differences between Japanese and British children in attributing natural objects to God
"The most striking finding was that Japanese children, despite being brought up in a culture that is essentially non-theistic, still showed a propensity to see a creator God behind the natural world." — Dr. Olivera Petrovich

Barrett's "Princess Alice" Experiment

Experimental Design:

Barrett's team studied 57 children (ages 3-8) to test how they conceptualize invisible agents with special powers.

Setup: Children were introduced to an invisible princess named "Princess Alice" who had special powers:

  • Could make things happen without touching them
  • Could see everything in the room
  • Could pass through solid objects
  • Knew what was in closed containers

Children were then given tasks to test if they truly conceptualized these abilities.

Test Example:

Researcher: "Princess Alice is sitting in that chair. She can see everything in this room."

Researcher: [Places toy behind a barrier] "Can Princess Alice see the toy?"

Child (age 4): "Yes, because she has special eyes that can see everything."

Researcher: "If I put this ball in this box and close it, does Princess Alice know what's in the box?"

Child: "Yes, she knows everything. She has magic powers."

Results:

Even with minimal description, children:

  • Readily accepted and reasoned about Princess Alice's superhuman qualities
  • Did not confuse her abilities with human limitations
  • Conceptualized her consistently with how they described God

Kelemen's Teleological Experiments

Experimental Design:

Kelemen studied children ages 4-10 to test their tendency toward teleological thinking (purpose-based reasoning).

Setup: Children were shown images of natural objects and asked about their purpose.

Materials:

  • Pictures of natural entities (clouds, mountains, rocks)
  • Pictures of artifacts (chairs, pencils)
  • Picture cards for response options

Controls: Different age groups, counterbalanced presentations

Example Question Format:

Researcher: [Shows picture of pointy rocks] "Why do you think these rocks are pointy?"

Child (age 5): "So animals can scratch on them when they get itchy."

Researcher: "Why do you think clouds are puffy and white?"

Child: "So that we can see them and know when it's going to rain."

Researcher: "Why do you think rivers exist?"

Child: "For animals to drink from and for fish to live in."

Key Results:

  • Children ages 4-8 consistently preferred teleological explanations for natural objects
  • Tendency to see design and purpose in nature emerged spontaneously
  • Children frequently referenced "someone" making natural things "for" a purpose
  • Even children from non-religious backgrounds showed teleological reasoning

Bayesian Analysis of Fitrah Evidence

Bayes' Theorem: P(F|E) = [P(E|F) × P(F)] / P(E)

Where:

  • F = "The Islamic concept of fitrah is true - humans have an innate disposition to believe in Allah"
  • E = Evidence from developmental psychology, cross-cultural studies, etc.
  • P(F|E) = Probability that fitrah is true given our evidence
  • P(E|F) = Probability we would observe this evidence if fitrah is true
  • P(F) = Prior probability that fitrah is true
  • P(E) = General probability of observing the evidence

Key Points:

1. If fitrah is true (F), we would expect to find exactly the evidence (E) we do: children across cultures naturally forming concepts of a creator Allah/God.

2. P(E|F) is extremely high - the evidence is precisely what we'd predict if fitrah is true.

3. P(E|¬F) is very low - without fitrah, it's difficult to explain why children universally form these concepts.

4. Therefore, the evidence strongly confirms fitrah - P(F|E) >> P(F)
